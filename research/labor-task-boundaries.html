Reshaping Labor Task Boundaries after AI Adoption

Abstract

This paper studies why, conditional on AI adoption, organizations arrive at different configurations of AI augmentation and AI automation across task structures. I develop a continuous task-chain model that places task-chain continuity, judgment non-substitutability, and new-task creation elasticity within a single augmentation-automation boundary problem. The model shows that greater task-chain continuity expands the automation interval; greater judgment non-substitutability expands the augmentation interval; and greater new-task creation elasticity expands the augmentation interval while raising conditional labor input within the AI-adopted task domain. The central theoretical result is a reversal in the labor effect of AI capability improvements: when capability improvements mainly translate into relative automation cost savings, conditional labor input falls; when they translate more strongly into new-task returns, conditional labor input rises. I further endogenize new-task creation and show that augmentation effects depend not only on task opportunities but also on organizational adjustment costs. The paper does not directly explain changes in aggregate industry employment. Instead, it explains the task-structure boundary mechanism behind heterogeneity across industries, occupations, and workflows. A finite task-chain simulation is provided as a supplementary mechanism in the appendix.
